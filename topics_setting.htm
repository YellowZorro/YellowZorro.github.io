Setting

Here at MiniLuv, we pride ourselves in our ability to keep our citizens true to the proper principles of Ingsoc, without carelessly sacrificing too many lives. Our methods of torture that we have developed over the years are so effective because we combine physical and psychological pain with an environment that instills hopelessness. When we place our troublesome prisoners into a “high-ceilinged windowless cell with walls of glittering white porcelain” (Orwell 285) they feel trapped, they know we control them, they lose all hope. And hope is the biggest enemy of the Party, for hope, if allowed to ripen, is the only thing that can turn our people against us.

               

Our enemies and allies around the world use similar techniques to keep their own citizens in check. The ISIS group in Eurasia has made especially great use of their location recently. While we use enclosed rooms to set the scene and eliminate chance of escape, they use the vast emptiness of the desert. Their prisoners are threatened by death every day, but what really brings them down into submission is the fact that they are being held captive in the middle of a desert. (Beirut 7) Nowhere to run. No chance of escape. Nobody to help them. These are the same feelings we try to instill in our own prisoners, and this is why we are both so effective in spreading fear and maintaining control of huge groups of people.

At MiniLuv we also take pride in making sure that even the tiniest details serve our purposes. From the “concealed lamps” that let our prisoners see without knowing where the gift is coming from, to the “low, steady humming” (Orwell, 285) that serves to intrude into any independent thoughts and to reduce thoughtcrime in our facilities, every piece of our perfect design is finely calculated to maximize our influence on the minds of our subjects. One of our allies, the Democratic People’s Republic of Korea, also does as very good job of this. They have managed to attain, as we have, a complete isolation of their society from the outside world. This would not have been possible without perfectly monitoring the surroundings of each individual in the country. No unmonitored media, no travel opportunities outside of the country, and no signs of poverty may be allowed to manifest in society. Whether it is a North Korean citizen or someone looking in from outside, there is “no reference to the hunger suffered by all but a privileged elite in the party, …, no mention of the North’s reliance upon [others] for food and other aid, ..., no passing allusion to the lack of medical care, no hint of the suffering of the country and its people.” (Kirk) The environment the citizens live in becomes a form of torture in itself, for while people may be dissatisfied with their situation, they see no alternatives - no escape.

George Orwell writes so clearly regarding the environments throughout 1984 because they play such an important part in setting the mood of the characters in the story. Winston and other characters were not only tortured while imprisoned and interrogated, but throughout their everyday lives. Even small things like the electricity cutting off during the day (Orwell 3) or the widespread portraits of Big Brother (Orwell 4) serve to slowly wear away at the resolve of even the most rebellious individuals. And many of these examples come shockingly close to societies outside of Oceania. Even in America, the beacon of freedom in today’s world, “The poorer quarters [are] swarmed with women who were ready to sell themselves,” (Orwell 83) just as they are in 1984. Orwell draws many of these parallels between the world of Winston and the world outside in order to show just how close we are, just how easy it would be for us to descend into a similar situation. The environment that people are in is the easiest thing for a government or any ruling power to control - and once the daily lives of a population feel like torture, the government is free to act it pleases.
