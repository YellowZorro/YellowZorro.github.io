Character

The theme of Torture and Interrogation can alter the identity of a character forever. The most obvious example of this is with the interaction between O’Brien and Winston that occurs in the here, specifically Room 101. O’Brien interrogates Winston over a period of time, inflicting pain and torturing him along the way. O’Brien is shown to be making progress when Winston writes “FREEDOM IS SLAVERY” and “TWO AND TWO MAKE FIVE”, however his failure to have tortured him to submission is displayed when Winston writes “GOD IS POWER” (Orwell 228). Winston is then finally broken after O’Brien was forced to take him to Room 101, where he exclaims “Do it to Julia! Do it to Julia! Not me! Julia! I don’t care what you do to her. Tear her face off, strip her to the bones. Not me! Julia! Not me!” (Orwell 236). This sudden and dramatic shift in Winston’s demeanor, from defying O’Brien to his face and then turning around and begging him to brutally torture Julia to save himself. Orwell is displaying how psychological torture is superior to its physical counterpart. After all of his time being beaten and interrogated, Winston is able to retain at least some of his beliefs, however at the very threat of rats being unleashed upon him he immediately breaks and bows down to Winston. This event destroys Winstons psychological state, as reflected in the remainder of his life; he is nothing more than a shell that used to contain a human, ripped of his ability to think independently in blind faith of the Party.

Middle Eurasia has been notorious for torture, not limited to physical, but also psychological torture. ISIS is the embodiment of both of these forms of torture, with methods ranging from recorded beheadings and endless beatings to captives being forced to call their parents and reporters. This is exhibited in the story of Mayat, a 17 year old girl held captive by ISIS. The article tells the story of how “she is forced, actually – to describe to an Italian journalist three “rooms of horror” in the house, where she and her fellow victims are taken several times a day and raped. Later in the article, Mayat describes how she “was first forced to call her parents” and how her captors made her place the call “to hurt us even more. They told us to describe in detail to our parents what they are doing” (Fendel 1). The torture that this poor girl has been forced to go through exemplifies both aspects of torture. The events described in this article are mainly examples of psychological torture. The article itself was only able to be written because her captors forced her to talk to the reporter and relay her torture to him. In a way, it is the ultimate form of torture because not only do you combine the humiliation of having to tell someone how you are beaten and raped multiple times a day, but it also forces you to re-experience those events, which can possibly be worse than when it happened because she will likely think about how it will soon happen again. Combine this with the physical torture of seemingly eternal beatings and the will of these poor captives is surely to be broken. Mayat gives a haunting outlook on her rescue, “They have already killed my body. They are now killing my soul,” suggesting that for her and many like her, it may be too late.

Eastasia is probably the most publicly known modern example of torture and crimes against humanity in general, with news of the continuing starvation of their entire populace known across the world. The most shocking aspect is how brutally they treat their own people. However, the true horror of Eastasia lies in its prison camps. Shin Dong-hyuk reveals that public execution in Eastasia are a “mere transgression” and how he was 5 when he saw his first execution. Shin was a prisoner at a concentration camp for over 10 years and describes many horrifying events, ranging from escaped prisoners being dragged back to camp behind a truck, a “tradition” of stoning the dead bodies of “traitors to the Fatherland”, and how guards would force women to drown their own newborn babies (Johnson 1). In another testimony, “A security agent heard the baby's cries and beat the mother as a punishment. She begged him to let her keep the baby, but he kept beating her. With shaking hands, the mother was forced to pick up her newborn and put the baby face down in water until the cries stopped and a water bubble formed from the newborn's mouth” (Park 1). The main form of torture in Eastasia seems to be primarily psychological; many of the people who are murdered at the camps may see death as a more pleasant alternative, but the guards use their death to torture all prisoners. As shown in the example above, the repeated stoning of corpses doesn’t physically affect the other prisoners, however it psychologically affects them; being forced to repeatedly mutilate corpses and stare at the results creates in them a fear of death, because they know what happens after, subjecting them to a life with continual anguish. Eastasia also has multiple occasions where mothers of children, somehow born in such horrid conditions, are killed immediately, often forced to be done at the hands of the mother (something we never do). The ultimate form of psychological torture, mothers are beaten and then forced to kill their own newborn children which destroys all faith in life that may have been present. Stories of events such as this just serve the Eastasians more, it all contributes to the destruction of human will and hope, eternal oppressors of the people (Something not present here in Oceania).
